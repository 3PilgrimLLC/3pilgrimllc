Negative Tomography: Structural Inference via Failure-First Primitivess

A Companion Explainer

1) Why This Paper Exists

Most inference systems—human or machine—start the same way:
generate possibilities, test them, keep what works.

That approach works fine when the space is small, well-structured, or richly informative. But as spaces grow large, sparse, high-entropy, or effectively unbounded, positive-first inference starts to fail in predictable ways:

  • Possibilities explode combinatorially.

  • Early partial matches lock in the wrong structure.

  • Search effort grows faster than information gained.

  • Absence of evidence is ignored instead of treated as meaningful.

In these regimes, “trying harder” or “searching longer” doesn’t help. The strategy itself is wrong.

This paper starts from a simple observation:

In sparse spaces, absence carries more information than presence.

Negative Tomography exists to formalize that idea—and build an inference framework around it.

2) The Core Idea (Plain English)

Instead of asking “What could this be?”, Negative Tomography asks:

“What can this definitely not be?”

Rather than generating candidates and pruning them reactively, we begin by exhaustively characterizing impossibility.

Each failure, non-occurrence, or forbidden configuration eliminates not just one option, but an entire class of possibilities. Collect enough of these “negative primitives,” and what remains is no longer vague—it becomes sharply defined.

In other words:

  • Positive inference builds outward.

  • Negative inference collapses inward.

And in large or degenerate spaces, collapse is vastly more efficient than expansion.

3) Negative and Positive Primitives

Any inference problem can be described using two dual sets:

  • Positive primitives: minimal generators of allowed states.

  • Negative primitives: minimal forbiddances—things that cannot occur under any circumstances.

Most methods prioritize positives and treat negatives as constraints, penalties, or afterthoughts.

Negative Tomography inverts that priority.

Why?

Because in sparse or unbounded spaces:

  • Negative sets are usually smaller.

  • Each negative carries more information.

  • Failure collapses the space faster than success expands it.

Positive structure still matters—but only as the complement of fully satisfied negatives.

4) Failure as a Structural Signal

In this framework, failure is not noise. It is signal.

A single failure doesn’t just say “this attempt didn’t work”—it tells us that an entire region of the space is impossible.

Different kinds of failure reveal different structure:

  • Hard failures define absolute boundaries.

  • Systematic absences expose missing degrees of freedom.

  • Repeated failures under variation reveal invariants.

  • Failures of attempted fixes point to deeper constraints.

Negative Tomography treats these as atomic, non-negotiable primitives—the raw material of inference.

5) Why Symmetry Always Appears at the End

As negative primitives accumulate, the feasible space contracts. Across domains, this contraction has a consistent outcome:

The surviving structure becomes maximally symmetric.

This isn’t aesthetic—it’s structural.

Symmetry here means:

  • Minimal degrees of freedom

  • Maximal invariance

  • Stability under perturbation

  • No arbitrary asymmetries not enforced by constraints

This explains a familiar phenomenon: solutions discovered through long failure-first processes often look “obvious” in hindsight. They seem simple because everything else has been proven impossible.

Simplicity is not assumed—it’s what remains.

6) Why This Is Universal

Negative Tomography isn’t tied to any one field. The same invariants show up everywhere:

  • Physical sensing: shadows, silence, and non-reflection define shape.

  • Language: forbidden placements constrain meaning faster than guesses.

  • Probability: zero-probability transitions define dynamics.

  • Optimization: flatness and degeneracy signal constrained manifolds.

  • Theory-building: impossibility theorems bound what can exist.

The claim is strong but precise:

Any method that successfully infers structure in sparse or unbounded spaces must—implicitly or explicitly—use negative primitives.

Different techniques often turn out to be the same strategy in disguise.

7) Design Invariants (Without Algorithms)

This paper does not prescribe implementations. But any valid realization of Negative Tomography must respect five invariants:

  1. Failure-first ordering – impossibility precedes construction.

  2. Non-pegging – provisional structures remain revisable.

  3. Anchor acquisition – inference starts only after at least one informative negative boundary exists.

  4. Recursive inversion – failed fixes are treated as higher-order signals.

  5. Convergence by relaxation – stability emerges by releasing unnecessary constraints, not enforcing all of them.

These define what the method must be, not how to code it.

8) What This Paper Is—and Is Not

This document is foundational, not procedural.

It:

  • Defines why failure-first inference is necessary.

  • Introduces a minimal vocabulary and structure.

  • Provides a unifying lens across domains.

It does not:

  • Offer algorithms

  • Present benchmarks

  • Claim optimality in every setting

Those belong in future, domain-specific work.

Bottom Line

Negative Tomography reframes inference as a process of eliminating impossibility until only structure remains. In sparse, high-entropy, or degenerate spaces, this is not a clever trick—it is the only strategy

that scales.

Where positive-first methods search blindly, failure-first inference lets the space collapse itself.
