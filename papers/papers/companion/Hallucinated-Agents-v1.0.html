Hallucinated Agents: A Cognitive Mechanism for Resolving Ambiguous Threats

A Companion Explainer

3 Pilgrim LLC

Version 1.0 · February 12, 2026

We've all felt it at some point. As a kid lying in bed after the lights go out, hearing a creak in the hallway and suddenly convinced -something- is there. Or walking alone at night and catching a flicker of movement in the corner of your eye. Or camping in the woods, a rustle makes your heart slam and your mind scream “run” before you even know why.

Your world narrows instantly.

Every sound is amplified… shadows deepen… the air feels thick.

Your pulse surges, muscles tighten, and your thoughts scramble: What was that? Am I in danger?

In this instant Fear is doing real work. It’s priming you for survival before you have time to think.

The Dual‑Prep Defensive Cascade: Upon a fear trigger, the brain launches two parallel processes in tens of milliseconds:

1. Perceptual Gain ↑ (amygdala → sensory cortices)

2. Motor/Metabolic Priming ↑ (amygdala → PAG → sympathetic surge: HR↑, contractility↑, catecholamines↑)

Freeze/orient/flee transitions are mediated by amygdala–PAG pathways. This happens before conscious interpretation.

When the World Goes Ambiguous

When a threat stimulus is ambiguous—too fast, too vague—your brain is faced with an existential dilemma. It can’t immediately sort “harmless” from “lethal.” In this moment of uncertainty, something remarkable happens: the mind often injects a false presence. A fictional agent: a shadowy figure in the dark. Footsteps behind you. A shape that vanishes when you turn.

The question is: why does terror produce agent‑like hallucinations rather than abstract blobs or meaningless sensations?

Ambiguity Collapse & Agentive Completion: Under high ambiguity, humans default to intentional explanations (“something is there”) rather than abstract ones (“something exists”). This is the well‑documented tendency called hyperactive agency detection (HADD) in cognitive science, especially pronounced under threat. Your brain isn't “adding details” — it's inserting minimal structure (an orienting reference object) to make action computable.

The Core Insight: The Mind’s Circuit‑Breaker

The paper argues this hallucination isn’t superstition or malfunction. It’s a cognitive circuit‑breaker: when fear + urgency + ambiguity hit a critical threshold, the system forces directionality by inserting a Minimal Viable Agent (MVA).

An MVA is not a detailed hallucination, it’s the cheapest possible adversarial outline: “something is there” and it has intent and it has direction. That’s enough information for your motor system to lock onto a policy: avoid, orient, flee, or inspect.

the Bounded‑Time Controller

The model formalizes this with a dual‑threshold stop rule:

τ* = maximum safe time to remain unresolved

λ* = load threshold (fear/arousal)

When Ambiguity → 1, Fear → high, and time > τ*, evaluating further becomes unsafe.

The controller inserts an MVA to terminate the loop. This implements Error Management Theory: false positives are safer than false negatives in survival contexts.

Why Evolution Built It This Way

Our ancestors lived in environments where hesitation got you killed quick. Rustling leaves might be wind… or a predator. A creature that freezes indefinitely becomes easy prey. Evolution could not allow a brain that stalls in uncertainty when physiology is spiraling upward. So, the system evolved a fail‑fast mechanism: when ambiguity remains high and the sympathetic system is already “red‑lined,” the brain shoves structure into the void. Accuracy is unimportant in the moment; provoking action is the purpose.

Physiological Hazard Under Ambiguity

Sustained sympathetic load (catecholamines, HR↑, contractility↑) becomes physiologically risky if prolonged — associated with autonomic instability, arrhythmia, and rare cases like stress cardiomyopathy (Takotsubo) triggered by acute emotional shock. This isn’t the foundation of the model — it’s the boundary condition showing why termination matters. A sustained loop is by itself physically dangerous.

Internal Threats: Panic Loops / Panic Attacks

When fear turns inward (“My heart is racing — am I dying?”), the threat has no external location. The ambiguity becomes self‑referential.

The loop accelerates: fear → interoceptive monitoring → catastrophic interpretation → more fear → stronger sensations → worse interpretation.

This is a runaway recursive process because the organism is both detector and detected.

Internal Off‑Ramp (IMVC)

Unlike external triggers, internal loops don’t readily invoke an MVA. Instead, the system requires an Internal Minimal Viable Cause (IMVC) — a benign interpretation (“this is adrenaline,” “this will pass”) that collapses catastrophic appraisal. Clinical panic treatments (interoceptive exposure, re‑labeling) are engineered versions of this natural mechanism.

Why Do We Invoke Takotsubo in the Paper

Takotsubo is not common, not typical, and not central to the theory. It is included as a boundary signal: it shows us what is possible in the extremes. It is evidence that recursive stress escalation can surpass organ-level tolerance. Further, the rarity of this phenomenon the supports the model. That this is not more common, suggests the circuit‑breaker usually works.

Why This Matters to the Architecture

Takotsubo is mechanistically linked to excessive sympathetic discharge during acute emotional stress. It demonstrates the upper bound of what happens when termination mechanisms fail, not what typically occurs.

The Survival Logic Behind Hallucinated Agents

Those childhood monsters, alleyway shadows, and forest presences are not glitches. They’re evolution’s safety valve: give the unknown a face → force orientation → restore agency → demand action. This is the system protecting you from getting stuck in a fatal freeze.

Action Surface Re‑Sloping

Ambiguity flattens the “action surface” — no option looks safer than any other. An MVA re‑slopes that surface, making directional action computable again. The math appears in the main paper (risk function R(t)).

Technical Conclusion

The model treats fear not as an emotion alone but as a bounded‑time control problem inside a biologically constrained system.

When ambiguity stays high under rising arousal, the defensive cascade enters a configuration where continued evaluation becomes physiologically expensive, computationally unstable, and evolutionarily unsafe.

The system must resolve the uncertainty before sympathetic load crosses thresholds associated with autonomic instability or, in extreme outlier cases, stress‑induced cardiomyopathy (Takotsubo).

To prevent recursive collapse, cognition employs minimal structural insertion:

  • MVA for external ambiguity (a fictive adversary sufficient to restore an action vector), and

  • IMVC for internal ambiguity (a benign explanation sufficient to collapse catastrophic interoception).

These insertions function as termination primitives—faulty‑but‑functional mechanisms that enforce bounded time‑to‑decision under asymmetric risks, echoing the logic of Error Management Theory where false positives are systematically safer than false negatives under threat.

By reframing “hallucinated agents” and panic‑related misinterpretations as architectural responses, the model unifies phenomena typically treated as unrelated: freeze persistence, agentive shadows at the perceptual edge, panic episodes, catastrophic interoception, and the rare physiological boundary of Takotsubo. It grounds them in one principle: when the action surface collapses under ambiguity, the system must forcibly re‑slope it to survive.

The result is a testable, falsifiable account of how humans manage acute uncertainty under load. The model predicts modality effects, interoceptive vs. exteroceptive divergence, individual differences (IU, Type M/P), and specific patterns in VR‑based threat paradigms. It is a structural theory, not a clinical one: a proposal about how the architecture terminates dangerous loops, not what any given episode means.

Understanding these mechanisms reframes fear‑driven hallucination and panic not as pathology but as adaptive timing solutions—primitive, rapid, and often invisible. And by making the architecture explicit, it opens the door to new interventions, new predictions, and new ways to think about cognition under extreme constraint.
